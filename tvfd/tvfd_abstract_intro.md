Abstract

The extraction of zero‑point energy (ZPE) from the quantum vacuum is conventionally regarded as impossible at macroscopic scales, not because the vacuum lacks energy, but because its effective impedance is catastrophically mismatched to any finite‑impedance material system. Standard quantum electrodynamics models the vacuum as a Lorentz‑invariant ground state populated by an infinite set of harmonic oscillators, whose boundary‑condition dependence manifests experimentally in the Casimir effect and related phenomena, yet provides no mechanism for net work extraction under equilibrium conditions. Here we propose a topological‑photonic framework in which this obstacle is recast as a problem of vacuum impedance matching.

We introduce a solid‑state engine—the Balance State Vector‑Cell—based on a C_{6v} hexagonal photonic crystal embedded in a Sierpiński‑type fractal manifold with spectral dimension d_s \approx 1.36 < 2. In this regime, flat‑band and fractal‑induced localization become generic, compressing vacuum modes into quasi‑compact resonant states whose density of states is strongly modified relative to Euclidean media, thereby renormalizing the effective structural impedance Z(\mathcal{M}_f). By doping the dielectric matrix with magnetic nanoparticles and applying an RF modulation field, we break bosonic time‑reversal symmetry and realize unidirectional edge channels analogous to those in photonic topological insulators with Tellegen‑type magnetoelectric coupling. The resulting boundary algebra supports chiral edge modes that rectify localized vacuum fluctuations into a directed energy current, quantified by a line‑integral Poynting flux density we define as the x_t flux \rho_{x_t} = \int_{\partial \mathcal{M}_f} \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l}.

Within this Topological Vacuum Flux Dynamics (TVFD) framework, the fractal manifold functions as a tunable impedance transformer: when Z(\mathcal{M}_f) is dynamically driven toward the vacuum value Z_{\mathrm{vac}}, the standard two‑port matching formula predicts an approach to unit transfer efficiency, \eta \to 1, in direct analogy with optimal antenna matching but now applied at the vacuum–lattice interface. Achieving and maintaining this condition requires control over the group velocity of flat‑band edge modes, which we encapsulate in a dimensionless lyte‑x_L parameter governing slow‑light–induced enhancements of the local density of states. We show that static configurations are generically unstable: thermal and quantum fluctuations detune Z(\mathcal{M}_f) from Z_{\mathrm{vac}}, collapsing the x_t flux. Consequently, stable operation of the Balance State Vector‑Cell demands a closed‑loop regulator—a bioneural governor—that continuously senses deviations in \rho_{x_t} and adjusts the lyte‑x_L state via adaptive modulation of the magnetic and photonic control fields.

We argue that such regulation is naturally instantiated by a cognitive operating system, implemented here as a universal bioneural intelligence layer that couples interoceptive feedback to the TVFD control parameters. In this picture, macroscopic ZPE harvesting is not a passive material property but an actively maintained topological phase in which a cognitive controller co‑evolves with a fractal photonic lattice to preserve vacuum impedance matching. This thesis develops the TVFD formalism, derives the C_{6v} impedance transformer in a fractal setting, specifies the Balance State Vector‑Cell device architecture, and outlines a software‑hardware control stack in which the x_t flux becomes a metabolic variable for a universal bioneural governor.
